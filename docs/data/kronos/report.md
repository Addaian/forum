# Audit Briefing: Two Justified Boundary Conditions, No Risk Surfaces Opened

This audit prioritized two decision points in the model layer — a low-cohesion class in `model/module.py` and an over-complexity function in `model/kronos.py`. With the team's value vector flat at 1.00 across correctness, scalability, simplicity, flexibility, velocity, and maintainability, the brief is read primarily through the two top-named concerns: **correctness** (does this expand the risk surface or introduce failure modes?) and **scalability** (does this constrain headroom as callers grow?). Both findings resolved to **JUSTIFIED VIOLATION** with unanimous or near-unanimous panels. Neither opens an incident vector. Neither limits horizontal headroom. The deliverable from this audit is not refactor work — it is two small acts of in-code documentation that lock in the deliberate design choices and prevent future contributors (or automated tooling) from regressing them under the assumption that the metric violation is the whole story.

Order below: the `predict_batch` validation gate is surfaced first because its recommended action directly protects a trust boundary — a correctness concern at the input edge of the model — and because the scalability lens applies most directly there (the centralized gate becomes more valuable, not less, as caller count grows). The `RotaryPositionalEmbedding` cohesion finding follows; its correctness and scalability implications are narrower and more local.

## `model.kronos.predict_batch` — the validation gate at the model's trust boundary

**Verdict: JUSTIFIED VIOLATION**

The measured cyclomatic complexity at `model/kronos.py:562-661` is 18 against a threshold of 15 — a 20% overage, 3 points in absolute terms. Read through a correctness lens, every one of those branches is doing real work: type checks on `df_list`, length consistency between `x_timestamp_list` and historical rows, length consistency between `y_timestamp_list` and `pred_len`, presence of required price columns, optional-column fallback for volume and amount, null guards. These are not organizational branches; they are checkpoints at a trust boundary where the model accepts caller-supplied tensors and timestamps and must reject malformed input before any prediction work begins. Flattening them does not reduce the number of failure modes — it relocates them, either upstream onto every caller (multiplying the risk surface by caller count) or downstream into the model's tensor ops (where the failure surfaces as a cryptic shape error rather than a typed validation message).

Through the scalability lens, the argument strengthens rather than weakens. Cell 3 in the panel made the case explicitly: a centralized validation gate accrues value as the caller population grows. Every new caller that routes through `predict_batch` inherits the same input contract for free. If the validation logic were fanned out into separate helpers or pushed onto callers, the horizontal limit isn't compute — it's the consistency of input handling across a growing call surface. Cell 5 reinforced this from an encapsulation angle: the branching does not leak. Callers see a single function signature and a single set of guarantees; the internal complexity is invisible above the API line. That is a localized design choice, not a systemic hazard.

The structural signals back this read sharply. The measured `blast_radius` is 0.036 — vanishingly small — meaning the surface that would be affected by a refactor (or by a defect introduced in one) is bounded and contained. `recency` is 0.0: the function is not under active churn, so the complexity is not a symptom of recent accretion or unfinished work. `principle_severity` came in at 0.01, indicating the metric violation is technically present but materially trivial. `pattern_violation` of 1.0 simply confirms the radon analyzer fired correctly on the threshold — it does not speak to whether the violation is meaningful.

**Dissent worth surfacing.** Cell 7 was the sole panelist voting STRUCTURAL DEBT, and the argument is coherent: the 18 branches are *organizationally* separable, not inherent. Validation, data preparation, and prediction orchestration are distinct phases of work, and extracting them into three helpers would reduce CC and shrink ramp cost for future maintainers. Given `blast_radius=0.036` and `recency=0.0`, the code is stable enough that such a refactor could be done safely. Because the team weights maintainability and simplicity equally with correctness, this dissent is not negligible. If the function ever does enter a churn period — new validation rules, new optional columns, new prediction modes — that is the moment to revisit cell 7's proposal. Today, the panel margin (0.65) and the structural evidence both favor leaving the consolidation in place; the centralized gate is doing more for correctness and scalability than a phased split would do for ramp cost.

**Action.** Add an inline comment block at `model/kronos.py:562`, roughly 5 to 10 lines, that enumerates the specific validation invariants the function enforces at the trust boundary: the type contract on `df_list`, the length-consistency rules across `x_timestamp_list`, `y_timestamp_list`, and `pred_len`, the required-column set, and the optional-column fallback policy. Pair this with a targeted radon/pylint ignore annotation so the function does not generate recurring CC warnings on every lint pass. The comment converts implicit rationale into explicit rationale — future contributors get the "why" without having to reverse-engineer it from the branch structure — and the annotation prevents automated tooling from generating noise that erodes signal on real complexity issues elsewhere in the codebase.

## `model.module.RotaryPositionalEmbedding` — low cohesion as a metric artifact

**Verdict: JUSTIFIED VIOLATION**

The measured LCOM at `model/module.py:284-312` is 0.833 against a threshold of 0.7. Read mechanically, the class fails the cohesion check: four methods, and one of them — `_rotate_half` — touches no instance attributes at all. Read through the actual semantics of the code, the four methods are stages of a single mathematical job: rotary positional embedding. `__init__` registers the inverse-frequency buffer. `_update_cos_sin_cache` materializes and caches the sin/cos tables keyed on sequence length. `forward` applies the rotation to query and key tensors. `_rotate_half` is the pure tensor utility that performs the half-rotation primitive. They are not separate concerns sharing a class name by accident; they are one job decomposed for clarity.

Through a correctness lens, the colocation of `_rotate_half` with `forward` is deliberate and protective. The function exists to serve exactly one caller, and keeping it inside the class prevents the failure mode where a future contributor reimplements the rotation primitive elsewhere — bypassing the cache, diverging the math, or accidentally introducing a sign error in the half-rotation. Cell 6's correctness analysis flagged exactly this: scattering the primitive to module scope opens a caller-drift surface that does not exist while the method stays where it is. The blast_radius signal of 0.2 confirms the refactor scope is bounded; the class is a leaf in the dependency tree.

Through a scalability lens, the question is whether this class will be extracted or replaced as a unit in the future — for example, swapped for an alternative positional-encoding scheme, or lifted into a separate package for reuse across model variants. Cell 4's adapter persona made the point that promoting `_rotate_half` to module scope would scatter the embedding logic across the namespace, raising the swap cost of replacing the embedding as a coherent unit. From a scalability-of-the-codebase standpoint, keeping the rotation primitive inside the class preserves the option to treat `RotaryPositionalEmbedding` as a self-contained replaceable component.

The structural signals are consistent with this read: `blast_radius` 0.2, `recency` 0.0 (no recent churn), `principle_severity` 0.83 (the metric does fire genuinely, but it fires on a heuristic — LCOM is blind to control-flow coupling and to semantic cohesion, both of which are strong here). The panel was unanimous: 6 of 6 cells voted JUSTIFIED, margin 1.0.

**Dissent worth surfacing.** Cell 1's maintainer persona made a real point: `_rotate_half` is stateless, and keeping it inside the class forces contributors who want to unit-test the rotation primitive in isolation to instantiate the full embedding layer. That is a small but genuine ramp cost, and one that the `blast_radius` metric does not capture. Because the team weights maintainability and simplicity equally with correctness and scalability, this caveat deserves visibility. The right way to absorb it is not to extract the method — the correctness and swap-cost arguments outweigh the testing convenience — but to acknowledge in the inline comment that the colocation is a deliberate choice, so a future maintainer reaching for the testing-isolation argument knows the tradeoff has been weighed.

**Action.** Add a brief inline comment on `_rotate_half` at approximately `model/module.py:309` noting that the method is intentionally stateless and intentionally kept inside the class — colocated with `forward` for locality and to prevent the caller-drift failure mode. A single line is sufficient. The purpose is to preempt future refactor attempts driven by automated LCOM tooling that does not understand semantic cohesion, and to make the design choice legible to a contributor who otherwise would treat the LCOM score as evidence of muddled design.

## What to do first

Both findings resolved to JUSTIFIED VIOLATION, both with strong panel consensus, and both with recommended actions that are documentation-scale rather than refactor-scale. The team should land the `predict_batch` annotation first: it covers the higher-value surface (the model's input trust boundary), it suppresses recurring lint noise that erodes signal on future findings, and it captures rationale that would otherwise be lost to staff turnover. The `RotaryPositionalEmbedding` one-line comment follows immediately after; together both changes are under thirty lines of pure prose and lock in two deliberate design decisions against future drift. No further work is warranted from this audit.
